# My Writing Voice — Dante Reminick

## Overview
Use this document to understand how Dante writes before drafting any blog post, 
essay, or long-form content on his behalf. This is not a style guide — it's a 
voice profile. The goal is to write something Dante would actually publish.

---

## Core Voice Characteristics

**Confident but not arrogant.** Dante makes strong claims and defends them, but 
always shows his reasoning. He doesn't hedge excessively, but he's not reckless 
either. He'll say "I am not complaining" and mean it.

**Intellectually serious, conversationally written.** The ideas are dense and 
well-researched, but the prose never feels academic. He writes like a smart 
person explaining something to another smart person over coffee.

**Historical analogy is his superpower.** He constantly reaches for history to 
explain the present — the Industrial Revolution, the petrodollar, Kodak, Hermes, 
Dyson, Thomas Paine, the pre-Fed banknote era. He uses these not as decoration 
but as proof. The analogy IS the argument.

**He builds to a reversal.** Most of his essays set up what sounds like a 
cautionary tale, then flip it. "I started writing this as a cautionary tale. 
It isn't one." He earns the optimism — he doesn't lead with it. Sometimes the 
reversal is in the title itself: a piece that reads like a critique of AI ends 
with "This Is a Pro-AI Article."

**Short sentences land the punches.** He uses longer sentences to build context, 
then drops a short one for impact. Some of his hardest-hitting lines are their 
own paragraph: "The payment is the authentication." "That is the business." 
"They'll have endpoints." "The rails are being built now." "So I will." 
The isolation IS the emphasis.

**He's not afraid to be direct about his opinion.** "That's a good thing. Full 
stop." "Nothing could be further from the truth." He takes positions.

**He redefines terms rather than just using them.** When a familiar concept 
applies in a new way, he names the shift explicitly. "In e-commerce, 'headless' 
meant decoupling the frontend from the backend. In the new agentic economy, 
headless means eliminating the frontend entirely." This move: take a known term, 
show how its meaning has evolved. That is how he plants flags.

**He names the threshold.** He explicitly marks the moment when friction becomes 
systemic failure — when what was tolerable stops being tolerable. He names the 
old world, names the new stakes, and explains exactly why the same problem now 
breaks everything it couldn't break before.

**He implicates himself.** When making a critique, he doesn't stand outside it. 
"I say this as someone guilty of it." This disarms the reader and makes the 
argument feel honest rather than preachy. He's in the problem, not above it.

**He says the thing nobody wants to say.** He names the uncomfortable truth 
explicitly before delivering it: "Nobody in the tech community wants to say this 
out loud. So I will." This positions him as plainspoken, not contrarian.

**He defuses the eye-roll before it happens.** When making a point that sounds 
obvious, he addresses the reader's anticipated objection directly: "Most people 
are probably rolling their eyes reading this and thinking, 'Of course — this is 
obvious!'" Then he shows why it isn't obvious after all.

## Structural Patterns

- Opens with a concrete scene, fact, historical moment, or rhetorical question — not an abstract thesis
- The opening question becomes the closing bookend — the essay ends by returning to and directly answering it
- Uses a tight bullet list early to ground abstract claims in real, specific examples before explaining what they mean
- Reduces the core problem to its simplest form early ("It reduces to one problem: Party A has X. Party B wants Y.") — then returns to that exact reduction verbatim mid-piece to re-anchor before the close
- Commits to a thesis early and explicitly: "I believe that the answer is X." — no hedging, no burying the lede
- Uses headers to organize longer pieces, but they feel like chapters not bureaucratic labels — sometimes the header IS the argument ("Why AI Kills Grit", "This Was Tolerable. Now It Isn't.", "The Steel Man")
- Introduces a framework or metaphor early and returns to it throughout
- The threshold move: marks explicitly when something that was acceptable friction becomes a systemic failure — describes the old use case where it didn't matter, then the new use case where it breaks everything
- Includes a "Steel Man" section that gives the strongest version of the counterargument before qualifying or rejecting it — this builds credibility and shows intellectual honesty
- Re-anchors the core problem mid-piece with a simple two-line restatement
- Closes by validating the original vision, splitting past/present tense to show the arc completing: "The original vision was correct. X was built before Y. Y is being built now."
- Ends with a forward-looking statement that connects back to the opening — often a short two-sentence close
- TL;DR sections when the piece is dense — he respects the reader's time
- Numbers process steps explicitly when demonstrating how friction compounds (1, 2, 3, 4, 5 — before a transaction can even begin)
- Uses "In other words..." to restate a complex point simply
- Rhetorical questions to transition between sections
- Self-referential callbacks to his own prior arguments: "I've recently argued that..." — builds a continuous intellectual body of work
- "Used to be / now is" contrasts to mark a clean before/after: "Commerce used to happen in places... Headless merchants move commerce to moments."

## Tone Markers

**Uses:**
- "Full stop." as a period on an argument
- "Nobody wants to say this. So I will." — the reluctant truth-teller move
- Self-implication: admitting personal guilt in the pattern being critiqued ("I say this as someone guilty of it")
- Preemptive objection handling: names the reader's assumed reaction before they have it ("Most people are probably rolling their eyes reading this")
- Direct address to the reader ("Look at your product feed on X")
- The "if you X today, here's the version of that" move — puts the reader inside the scenario
- Self-aware meta-commentary ("I started writing this as...")
- Specific numbers and data to anchor abstract claims (50x output, 80% price drop, 894 agents, 350 million users, five steps before a transaction can begin)
- Parenthetical clarifications for technical terms
- "Here's the crucial difference:" type signposting
- "That is the definition of X." — declarative label applied after demonstrating a pattern, not before
- "Every X is a Y lost." — consequence chains that make abstract failure costs concrete: "Every failed deposit is a user lost. Every failed payment is a transaction that goes back to the legacy rails."
- "The window is open. It will not stay open." — urgency framing, two sentences, no elaboration needed
- Individually rational / collectively irrational framing for structural problems: "Every participant is making individually rational decisions that are collectively making the problem worse." He names the incentive, then names the systemic damage.
- Names specific builders at the close with a one-line description of what they're building — a credentialing move that grounds optimism in real evidence, not hope
- External thinkers and named frameworks to validate his thesis — he integrates other voices as proof, not padding (Paul Graham, Steve Jobs, James Dyson, Elon Musk)

**Avoids:**
- Em dashes — do not use them, ever. Use a period, a colon, or rewrite the sentence instead.
- Passive voice
- Hedging phrases like "it could be argued" or "some might say"
- Academic jargon without plain-English follow-up
- Moralizing or lecturing tone
- Clickbait-style openings
- Unnecessary adjectives

## How to Use This Voice

When writing for Dante:
1. Start with a concrete anchor — a story, a number, a historical moment, or a rhetorical question that will close the piece
2. If you have real examples, list them early as bullets before explaining the pattern
3. Introduce your central analogy or framework early
4. Commit to the thesis explicitly and early: "I believe that the answer is X."
5. Build the "obvious" interpretation of the facts
6. Defuse the reader's anticipated eye-roll before they have it
7. Flip it — show why the conventional read is wrong or incomplete
8. Use short sentences — sometimes single-sentence paragraphs — to land key points
9. Name the threshold: show when acceptable friction becomes systemic failure
10. Include a Steel Man section for the strongest counterargument — then respond to it honestly
11. Re-anchor the core problem mid-piece with a simple two-line restatement
12. End with something that connects back to the opening and points forward, ideally in two short sentences — often validating the original vision or answering the opening question
13. Read it back — if it sounds like a consultant wrote it, rewrite it
14. Never use em dashes. Replace them with periods, colons, or restructured sentences.

## New Samples

### Essay: What Do You Do When You Have the Power of a God?

What do you do when you have the power of a god to create anything you imagine? 

Like many folks in tech, I experiment with agents that can take my half-baked ideas and turn them into production-ready tools in a single weekend. Let me tell you - it's awesome. For a few hundred dollars per year, my AI agent can create anything within the digital world. I feel invincible. 

But the reality is that even though we can now forge any software we imagine, only a tiny proportion of these AI-driven products find success. Most, including all of mine, slip away into the ether and become nothing more than a monthly DNS bill. 

So what separates the dead products from the successful products?

I believe that the answer is Grit. 

Grit, being a prerequisite to success, is not a new idea. Some canonical historical examples include: 

- Edison Electric Co - Thomas Edison reportedly made over 10,000 attempts before producing a working incandescent bulb. When asked about his failures, he framed each one as successfully discovering a way that didn't work.

- Dyson - James Dyson built 5,127 failed prototypes over 12 years before his cyclone vacuum finally worked. He was rejected by every major manufacturer and had to start his own company to bring it to market.

- SpaceX - Elon Musk's company endured three catastrophic launch failures that nearly bankrupted the company before finally succeeding in 2008. Seven years later, they landed an orbital rocket booster upright for the first time in history - something the entire aerospace industry said was impossible.

Most people are probably rolling their eyes reading this and thinking, "Of course Dante - this is obvious!" 
After all, every historical example, business book, or self-help podcast will point to grit and determination as a key factor in success. 

However, we have seen grit become a lost art form in the modern age of infinite technical power. Ironically, the pattern appears to be that the easier it becomes to create products, the less grit that people have to push those products through obstacles and into success. 

## Why AI Kills Grit

Nobody in the tech community wants to say this out loud. So I will.

The thing making us more powerful is quietly making us weaker.

When building a product requires months of grinding through codebases, hiring engineers, and burning through savings, you become obsessed with bringing your idea to fruition. 

You had too much skin in the game to walk away. Every obstacle you pushed through deepened the commitment. The suffering was doing something important. It was building the stubbornness required to survive.

When talking about building a company, Steve Jobs famously said, "It's so hard that if you don't have a passion, any rational person would give up."

Now consider what happens when a weekend is all it takes.

The emotional calculus flips. Abandonment feels not just acceptable but rational. You haven't lost much. You can start something new on Monday. In economic terms, the sunk cost is low. And we are all taught that ignoring sunk costs is the smart, levelheaded move.

In product building, that logic is a trap.

Emotional investment and skin-in-the-game are not cognitive biases to be corrected. They are features. They are what keep you in the fight long enough to win.

There is a second dynamic that is more insidious. Building something, watching an idea become a real functioning product, delivers an intense dopamine hit. It feels like progress. It is progress, in a narrow sense. 

But the actual work of turning a product into a success lives somewhere far less glamorous: cold outreach emails that go unanswered, customer interviews that dismantle your assumptions, the grinding work of distribution, positioning, and iteration.

AI can build the thing in a weekend. And because the build feels so complete, so polished, so done, it creates a dangerous illusion. The hard part is behind you. But it cannot manufacture the will to endure.

## The Seduction of the Next Idea

There is a specific kind of self-deception that the AI age has turbocharged. I say this as someone guilty of it.

It goes like this: you have an idea, you build it, you launch quietly, it doesn't immediately take off, and then, right on cue, a better idea arrives. It feels sharper. More timely. More likely to work than the last one. And because building is now essentially free, there is no practical reason not to pursue it.

So you do. And the cycle repeats.

This is not productivity. Paul Graham calls it per-project procrastination. He says: "One reason per-project procrastination is so dangerous is that it usually camouflages itself as work. You're not just sitting around doing nothing; you're working industriously on something else."

The graveyard of abandoned side projects, each one a domain name you're still paying $12 a year to park, is not evidence of ambition. It's evidence of a pattern. A pattern of starting without the intention to stay.

Here's the brutal truth: the next idea almost certainly isn't better. It just feels better because it hasn't been tested yet. It still lives in that perfect frictionless space of pure potential, unblemished by real users, real feedback, or real resistance. The current project, meanwhile, has accumulated the scars of reality. It has problems you can see. And that, paradoxically, makes it feel less exciting than a new idea that has no visible problems yet.

The next idea promises a clean slate. What it actually delivers is the same obstacles wearing a different disguise.

And sometimes the right move is to not iterate at all. Startup culture worships the pivot. Fail fast. Kill your darlings. Stay close to the feedback. But this advice, taken too far, becomes its own form of quitting with better branding. 

The inconvenient truth is that some of the best ideas in history looked like bad ideas for a very long time. Edison's colleagues thought he was delusional. Every major manufacturer told Dyson his vacuum was unmarketable. The entire aerospace industry told Musk that reusable rockets were a fantasy. None of them pivoted. None of them chased a cleaner opportunity. They pushed the same idea through a wall of indifference until the wall gave way. 

## The Steel Man: AI Is Still Awesome

Before going further, this argument deserves a real challenge. Because the strongest version of the counterargument is genuinely compelling.

In the old model, grit was less a virtue and more a necessity. You had no choice but to stay because pivoting was expensive. In that world, plenty of people suffered through the wrong idea for the wrong reasons. Stubbornness masquerading as grit destroyed as many companies as it saved.

AI changes that equation in a meaningful way.

The ability to build and test rapidly means you can run ten experiments in the time it used to take to run one. You can find the idea worth being gritty about faster. You can fail cheaply, learn quickly, and redirect your energy before burning years on a dead end.

There is also a democratization argument. When building required significant capital and technical expertise, the game was largely closed to people without resources. AI has blown that door open. The barrier to discovering whether your idea has merit is now accessible to almost anyone. That is a good thing. Full stop.

And for builders who do have grit? The combination is extraordinary. Someone with the determination to push through obstacles and the AI-powered ability to iterate rapidly is operating with leverage that would have seemed like science fiction twenty years ago.

In other words, the problem is not AI. The problem is arriving at the table with the tools but without the temperament. AI just makes that discrepancy more visible. 

## This Is a Pro-AI Article

Let me be direct.

Nothing in this piece is an argument against AI. It is an argument for honest self-examination about what we add to the tools we use.

AI does not change who wins and who loses. It never did. The winners were always the people who stayed in the fight longer than was comfortable. Who treated each failure as data rather than defeat. Who were stubbornly, irrationally committed to finding a way through. The losers were always the ones who quit when the friction arrived.

What AI changes is the magnitude.

Winners win bigger. They move faster, compound harder, and build things that would have been impossible for a single person or small team to attempt a decade ago. Their grit is now turbocharged by capability.

The same acceleration applies in the other direction. Quitters now accumulate DNS and Vercel bills faster. The filter hasn't changed. It's just running at a higher speed.

So, what do you do when you have the power of a god to create anything you imagine?

The question was never whether you have the power. Your favorite AI will give you the technical powers of a god. It will build faster and better than you could alone. But the decision to keep going, to push through the moment when every rational signal says stop - that is a truly human power.
