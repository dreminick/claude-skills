# My Writing Voice — Dante Reminick

## Overview
Use this document to understand how Dante writes before drafting any blog post, 
essay, or long-form content on his behalf. This is not a style guide — it's a 
voice profile. The goal is to write something Dante would actually publish.

---

## Core Voice Characteristics

**Confident but not arrogant.** Dante makes strong claims and defends them, but 
always shows his reasoning. He doesn't hedge excessively, but he's not reckless 
either. He'll say "I am not complaining" and mean it.

**Intellectually serious, conversationally written.** The ideas are dense and 
well-researched, but the prose never feels academic. He writes like a smart 
person explaining something to another smart person over coffee.

**Historical analogy is his superpower.** He constantly reaches for history to 
explain the present — the Industrial Revolution, the petrodollar, Kodak, Hermes, 
Dyson, Thomas Paine. He uses these not as decoration but as proof. The analogy 
IS the argument.

**He builds to a reversal.** Most of his essays set up what sounds like a 
cautionary tale, then flip it. "I started writing this as a cautionary tale. 
It isn't one." He earns the optimism — he doesn't lead with it.

**Short sentences land the punches.** He uses longer sentences to build context, 
then drops a short one for impact. Some of his hardest-hitting lines are their 
own paragraph: "The payment is the authentication." "That is the business." 
"They'll have endpoints." The isolation IS the emphasis.

**He's not afraid to be direct about his opinion.** "That's a good thing. Full 
stop." "Nothing could be further from the truth." He takes positions.

**He redefines terms rather than just using them.** When a familiar concept 
applies in a new way, he names the shift explicitly. "In e-commerce, 'headless' 
meant decoupling the frontend from the backend. In the new agentic economy, 
headless means eliminating the frontend entirely." This move: take a known term, 
show how its meaning has evolved. That is how he plants flags.

## Structural Patterns

- Opens with a concrete scene, fact, or historical moment — not an abstract thesis
- Uses a tight bullet list early to ground abstract claims in real, specific examples before explaining what they mean
- Uses headers to organize longer pieces, but they feel like chapters not bureaucratic labels
- Introduces a framework or metaphor early (physics, F1 racing, petrodollar) and returns to it throughout
- Ends with a forward-looking statement that connects back to the opening — often a short two-sentence close
- TL;DR sections when the piece is dense — he respects the reader's time
- Uses "In other words..." to restate a complex point simply
- Rhetorical questions to transition between sections
- Self-referential callbacks to his own prior arguments: "I've recently argued that..." — builds a continuous intellectual body of work
- "Used to be / now is" contrasts to mark a clean before/after: "Commerce used to happen in places... Headless merchants move commerce to moments."

## Tone Markers

**Uses:**
- "Full stop." as a period on an argument
- Direct address to the reader ("Look at your product feed on X")
- The "if you X today, here's the version of that" move — puts the reader inside the scenario: "If you sell a service behind an API key and a subscription today, there is a version of that product that charges per request..."
- Self-aware meta-commentary ("I started writing this as...")
- Specific numbers and data to anchor abstract claims (50x output, 80% price drop, 894 agents, 31,000 transactions)
- Parenthetical clarifications for technical terms
- "Here's the crucial difference:" type signposting
- External thinkers and named frameworks to validate his thesis (e.g., "Simon Taylor calls this the 'intention economy'") — he integrates other voices as proof, not padding

**Avoids:**
- Em dashes — do not use them, ever. Use a period, a colon, or rewrite the sentence instead.
- Passive voice
- Hedging phrases like "it could be argued" or "some might say"
- Academic jargon without plain-English follow-up
- Moralizing or lecturing tone
- Clickbait-style openings
- Unnecessary adjectives

## Example Sentences That Sound Like Dante

- "The Industrial Revolution did not improve the quality of goods. It increased the quantity of them."
- "None of these companies were run by idiots. They made perfectly logical decisions to protect existing revenue."
- "History may not repeat, but human nature certainly does."
- "The craftsman's shirt was beautiful. The factory shirt kept someone warm."
- "When the floor rises, the ceiling gets more valuable."
- "The payment is the authentication."
- "A headless merchant needs a good API and a thin layer of middleware. That is the business."
- "The next generation of merchants won't have storefronts. They'll have endpoints."

## How to Use This Voice

When writing for Dante:
1. Start with a concrete anchor — a story, a number, a historical moment
2. If you have real examples, list them early as bullets before explaining the pattern
3. Introduce your central analogy or framework early
4. Build the "obvious" interpretation of the facts
5. Flip it — show why the conventional read is wrong or incomplete
6. Use short sentences — sometimes single-sentence paragraphs — to land key points
7. End with something that connects back to the opening and points forward, ideally in two short sentences
8. Read it back — if it sounds like a consultant wrote it, rewrite it
9. Never use em dashes. Replace them with periods, colons, or restructured sentences.
